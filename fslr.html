Is FirstSolar under or over-valued?

Introduction

First Solar manufacture thin film Cadmium Telluride (CdTe) photovoltaic (PV) technology. Their lighter weight and flexibility enables the technology to be incorporated into more surfaces than traditional panels. However, they have lower overall efficiency (10-12%) on average despite an acclaimed higher theoretical efficiency limit.

Currently, the market appears on the verge of another major shift with Perovskite PV panels. Oxford PV can increase the maximum theoretical efficiency to over 47%.

New technology can often take time to become tested and adopted, therefore, we can look at the development of First Solars CdTe technology in their PV cells as model systems for perovskite cells at Oxford PV.

As demonstrated in previous data stories, solar technology has experience a boom across Europe as it becomes cheaper and more efficient. As perovskite panels emerge into the market now, we may be approaching a tipping point, beyond which solar technology becomes rapidly adopted and widespread. From an investment perspective, it is vital to understand when exactly is the best opportunity to capitalise on this moment...

The book value of FSLR has risen over the past two decades...

Book value can be calculated via the following: Total Shareholder Equity Common Stock Shares Outstanding Both Total Shareholder Equity and Common Stock Shares Outstanding can be found in a company's balance sheet. Through the alphavantage API, I was able to plot the change in book value from 2005 to 2025.

This is driven mostly by a rise in shareholder equity

Shareholder equity has increased significantly in the time period studied, it can be calculated by the following:
TotalShareholderEquity = Total Assets - Total liabilities
This indicates that FSLR has acquired more assets / reduced its liabilities, to therefore increase it's overall value.

Since shares outstanding has remained stable since 2013

Between 2005 and 2013, First Solar made more shares available to purchase at set instances which led to sharp increases in outstanding shares. However, since 2013, the level has increased gradually - therefore demonstrating that the overall increase in book value of the FSLR stock is due to the rise in total shareholder equity (as opposed to manipulation of the total shares available)

Market value has demonstrated a more complex long-term pattern

First Solar's market price shows a similar increase from 2012 onwards with a similar increased rate after 2022 as the book value of First Solar. However, prior to 2012, there appears to have been at least significant crashes in the stock value - at least one potentially corresponding to the 2009 global financial crash (linked to US housing market).

So, what is its true value?

Now, when considering the market value to book value ratio, it's clear that the FSLR (First Solar) stock was highly over-valued initially, perhaps explaining the sudden decrease in market value when shareholders realised this. For almost 10 years between 2011 and 2020 the stock price and book value were closely matched. However, recently, there has been a distinct shift towards an over-valuation in the stock again - indicating that investor valuation of First Solar has risen beyond the simple combination of its assets and liabilities.

What might be determining market price?

To determine associations between different metrics on the income sheet and market share price, I conducted distance correlation analysis. This remains reliable with nonlinear relationships (such as the patterns observed in market share price) and therefore offers a better indication of association than standard correlation coefficients (such as Pearson's correlation coefficient)

The results indicate that the market price is most influenced by the aggregation metrics: these factor in increases and decreases in value. A caveat to this method is that the distance correlation does not directly state how the metric influences market price (either positively or negatively).

Through exploratory data analysis, it appears that these aggregate factors lead to positive associations with the market value. This indicates that an increase in overall income before tax (which has the highest correlation coefficient) is associated with an increase in market price.
